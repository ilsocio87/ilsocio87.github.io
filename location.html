THE HISTORY

Without doubt the fanciest place we will ever have spent the night, Dalhousie Castle boasts a pretty impressive history and, we hope, a damn good breakfast.

Dalhousieâ€™s foundations and vaults date back to the 13th century, though much of the present structure was built in the mid-1400s. The distinctive red stone used to construct the walls was quarried from the banks of the South Esk River which flows through the castle grounds. Now a hotel, Dalhousie was previously owned by the Ramsay family, who held possession for longer than any other family has ever retained ownership of a Scottish castle.

Dalhousie witnessed many key (and grisly) moments in Scottish history, which was for centuries characterised by conflict with the dastardly English (absolutely no bias here!). The first Ramsay to own land at Dalwolsey (now Dalhousie) was reportedly Simundus de Ramesia, who followed King David I when he inherited the Scottish throne. King Edward I of England spent the night at Dalhousie before defeating William Wallace at the Battle of Falkirk (for an entertaining if somewhat inaccurate telling of this story, see Braveheart). William Ramsay later joined forces with Robert the Bruce, appearing with him at the Battle of Bannockburn and signing the Declaration of Arbroath.

Williamâ€™s son, Alexander, led a series of raids on the English which resulted in many occupied areas and castles being returned to Scottish ownership and was rewarded for his efforts by King David II, who made him Constable of Roxburgh and Sheriff of Teviotdale. This didnâ€™t go down too well with Alexanderâ€™s former friend, Sir William Douglas who ended Alexanderâ€™s lucky streak by having him imprisoned at Hermitage Castle where he starved to death in the dungeon. A later Alexander Ramsay resisted a six-month siege on the castle led by King Henry IV of England, and Dalhousie proved to be the last Scottish castle to be besieged by an English king in person. Later Earls of Dalhousie had close connections with King James VI, though the Civil War saw them change their allegiance to the side of the Parliamentarians, whose leader, Oliver Cromwell, used Dalhousie as his lowland headquarters. After the Act of Union in 1707 tensions between the Scots and the English gradually eased and the Ramsays turned their attentions from warfare to politics.

Today the castle maintains its tradition of hospitality in its capacity as a hotel, preserving much of the original character of the building, but with decidedly less bloodshed and some central heating and Wi-Fi thrown in for good measure.
